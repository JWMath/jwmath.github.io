Generalized Configuration space

In 2019, Professor Xuezhi zhao and I generalized Fadell's configuration space. We consider the subset M of vector space or projective space, defined generalized configuration space which is formed by n-tuples of elements in M where any k-elements of each n-tuple are linearly independent. Actually, this generalized configuration space also generalized Stiefel manifold. Based on this definition, we calculated its homotopy group, homology group, cohomology group. We also got some results on mapping from sphere to Euclid space. This space also give a defintion of the high-dimention braid group of projective space or vector space by its fundamental group.

This space corresponds Borsuk-Ulam theorem, Knaster's problem, arrangement of subspaces, singular theory and index theory.

The groups Gnk,Γnk

In 2015, Vassily O. Manturov formulated following fundamental principle:

If dynamical systems describing the motion of n particles posses a nice codimension one property governed by exactly k particles, then these dynamical systems admit a topological invariant valued in Gnk.

The first paper is:

Non-Reidemeister Knot Theory and Its Applications in Dynamical Systems, Geometry, and Topology

V.O.Manturov, I.M.Nikonov, S.Kim, D.A.Fedoseev have done many works about this. Most of their work can be found in their book:

On Groups Gnk and Γ nk: A Study of Manifolds, Dynamics, and Invariants

Around this, we can construct invariants and give new methods to study Knot, Braids,Links, surface, 3-manifold and so on.

Borsuk-Ulam theorem and Knaster's problem

Knaster's problem: In 1947, B.Knaster posed the following question:

Given a continuous function f mapping the (n-1)-dimentional Euclidean sphere Sn-1 ⊆ Rn into Rm, m ≤ n-1, and k=n-m+1 points p1,...,pk ∈ Sn-1, does there exist a rotation r ∈ SO(n) such that f(r(p1))=...=f(r(pk))?

There are some positive answers:

  • k=2; H.Hopf's work in 1944 answers the above question is true for k=2, thus this case generalized the Borsuk-Ulam theoremon antipodal point of spheres.
  • m=1,k=n=3; In 1955, E.E.Floyd proved Knaster's conjecture in Real-valued mappings of spheres.
On restrictions on the geometry of the set {p1,...,pk}:
  • m=1,k=n; H.Yamabe and Z.Yujobiô confirmed the conjecture if {p1,...,pk} is an orthonormal basis.
For general case, there are counterexpamles:
  • m=1,k∈{61,63,65}, or k≥67; A.Hinrichs and C.Richter in 2005;B.S.Kashin and S.J.Szarek in 2003;
  • m=2,k=3; W.Chen in 1998;
  • m=2,k≥4; B.S.Kashin and S.J.Szarek in 2003
  • m≥3,k=3; W.Chen in 1998;
  • m≥3,k≥4; V.V.Makeev in 1986; I.K.Babenko and S.A.Bogaty in 1989, W.Chen in 1998
Thus for Knaster's problem, there is two main question:
  • For general case, Knaster's question is open if m=1, 4≤k≤60 or k∈{62,64,60};
  • For the false case, What restrictions on the geometry of the set {p1,...,pk} or on the natrue of mapping f will make Knaster's conjecture true.
